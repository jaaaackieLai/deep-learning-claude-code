# Evidence Quality Assessment

## Overview

Evaluate the strength and quality of evidence systematically. Not all evidence is created equal—this guide helps you assess what conclusions are supported by what strength of evidence.

## When to Apply

- Weighing evidence for decisions
- Conducting literature reviews
- Comparing conflicting findings
- Determining confidence in conclusions

## Evidence Evaluation Framework

### 1. Study Design Hierarchy

**Traditional hierarchy (for intervention effects):**
1. Systematic reviews/meta-analyses (highest)
2. Randomized controlled trials
3. Cohort studies
4. Case-control studies
5. Cross-sectional studies
6. Case series/reports
7. Expert opinion (lowest)

**Important caveat:**
Higher-level designs aren't automatically better quality. A well-designed observational study can be stronger than a poorly-conducted RCT.

**Consider:**
- Design hierarchy is a starting point, not final judgment
- Quality within design type matters enormously
- Different questions favor different designs
- Ethical or practical constraints may preclude "ideal" designs

### 2. Quality Within Design Type

**Assessment tools:**
- **RCTs**: Cochrane Risk of Bias tool
- **Observational**: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale
- **Diagnostic**: QUADAS-2
- **Qualitative**: CASP checklist

**Universal quality indicators:**
- Low risk of bias
- Adequate sample size
- Appropriate analysis
- Complete reporting
- Transparent methods
- Conflicts of interest managed

### 3. GRADE Considerations

**GRADE** (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation) provides systematic approach to rating evidence quality.

**Starting point:**
- RCTs: Start as HIGH quality
- Observational: Start as LOW quality

**Downgrade for:**
1. **Risk of bias**: Methodological limitations
2. **Inconsistency**: Unexplained heterogeneity across studies
3. **Indirectness**: Wrong population, intervention, comparison, or outcome
4. **Imprecision**: Wide confidence intervals, small samples
5. **Publication bias**: Funnel plot asymmetry, missing studies

**Upgrade for (observational only):**
1. **Large effect**: Strong associations (RR > 2 or < 0.5)
2. **Dose-response**: Clear gradient
3. **Residual confounding**: Would reduce, not increase, effect

**Final rating:**
- **High**: Very confident true effect is close to estimate
- **Moderate**: Moderately confident; true effect likely close to estimate
- **Low**: Limited confidence; true effect may differ substantially
- **Very low**: Very little confidence; true effect likely substantially different

### 4. Convergence of Evidence

**Evidence is stronger when:**

**Multiple independent replications**
- Different research groups
- Different settings and populations
- Similar results across studies

**Different methodologies converge**
- RCTs and observational studies agree
- Different measurement methods
- Multiple analytical approaches

**Mechanistic and empirical evidence align**
- Biological plausibility
- Dose-response relationships
- Temporal sequence
- Experimental support for mechanism

**Evidence is weaker when:**
- Single study or research group
- Contradictory findings in literature
- Publication bias evident
- No replication attempts
- Mechanism unclear or implausible

### 5. Contextual Factors (Bradford Hill Criteria)

**Strength of association**
- Stronger associations less likely due to confounding
- Weak associations require more scrutiny

**Consistency**
- Replicated across populations, settings, methods
- Inconsistency raises questions (or suggests moderation)

**Specificity**
- Specific exposure-outcome pairs stronger than diffuse associations
- Less applicable in complex disease

**Temporality**
- Cause must precede effect (essential criterion)
- Reverse causation must be ruled out

**Biological gradient (dose-response)**
- Stronger evidence for causation
- Not always present (threshold effects)

**Plausibility**
- Biological mechanism makes sense
- Consistent with existing knowledge

**Coherence**
- Doesn't seriously conflict with known facts
- Fits with what else we know

**Experiment**
- Experimental evidence (RCTs, animal models)
- Removal of exposure reduces outcome

**Analogy**
- Similar cause-effect relationships exist
- Suggestive, not definitive

## Assessing Conflicting Evidence

**When studies disagree:**

1. **Compare quality**: Are differences due to methodological quality?
2. **Check populations**: Different populations, different results?
3. **Examine interventions**: Are treatments actually comparable?
4. **Review outcomes**: Same outcome measured the same way?
5. **Consider timing**: Early vs. late studies (methods improve)
6. **Look for bias**: Publication bias favoring positive results?

**Meta-analysis helps:**
- Pools evidence across studies
- Quantifies heterogeneity
- Identifies sources of variation
- But: Garbage in, garbage out

## Practical Application

### For a Single Study

1. Place in hierarchy (design type)
2. Assess quality within design type
3. Apply GRADE considerations
4. Check contextual factors
5. Determine confidence in findings

### For Multiple Studies

1. Assess quality of each study
2. Look for convergence vs. conflict
3. Consider heterogeneity sources
4. Synthesize evidence (narrative or meta-analysis)
5. Determine overall confidence

### For Evidence-Based Decisions

1. Define the question (PICO)
2. Search systematically
3. Assess each study's quality
4. Synthesize evidence
5. Grade overall quality
6. Make recommendation with strength rating

## Providing Feedback

**Structure:**

1. **Evidence Landscape**: Summarize available evidence
2. **Quality Assessment**: Rate quality of included studies
3. **Convergence**: Note agreement or disagreement
4. **Confidence**: State overall confidence level
5. **Gaps**: Identify what's missing

**Example:**
> "Evidence for this intervention comes from 3 RCTs (n=450 total) and 2 observational studies (n=2,300). RCT quality is moderate (allocation concealment unclear, high dropout in 1 study). Results are consistent showing 20-30% improvement (95% CI: 15-35%). Observational studies show similar effects but susceptible to confounding. GRADE rating: MODERATE. Confidence: Effect likely real, but magnitude uncertain. Gap: No long-term follow-up >6 months."
