# Claim Evaluation

## Overview

Systematically evaluate scientific claims for validity and support. Claims must be proportional to evidence, and evidence must be sufficient for claims.

## When to Apply

- Assessing conclusions in papers
- Evaluating media reports of research
- Reviewing abstract or introduction claims
- Checking if data support conclusions

## Claim Evaluation Process

### 1. Identify the Claim

**What exactly is being claimed?**
- State the claim explicitly
- Identify implicit assumptions
- Note the scope and specificity

**Claim type:**
- **Causal**: X causes Y
- **Associational**: X is related to Y
- **Descriptive**: X has property Y
- **Mechanistic**: X works through Y
- **Comparative**: X is better/worse than Y

**Claim strength:**
- **Proven/demonstrated**: Very strong
- **Likely/probable**: Strong
- **Suggested/indicated**: Moderate
- **Possible/may**: Weak
- **Speculative**: Very weak

### 2. Assess the Evidence

**What evidence is provided?**
- Direct vs. indirect evidence
- Primary data vs. citations
- Single study vs. multiple studies
- Quality of evidence

**Evidence-claim matching:**
| Claim Strength | Required Evidence |
|---------------|-------------------|
| "Proves" | Multiple high-quality RCTs, mechanistic support |
| "Demonstrates" | At least one well-designed study with replication |
| "Suggests" | Preliminary evidence, possible confounds |
| "May" | Weak evidence, highly preliminary |

**Sufficiency check:**
- Is evidence sufficient for claim strength?
- Are alternative explanations ruled out?
- Is evidence direct or circumstantial?

### 3. Check Logical Connection

**Do conclusions follow from data?**
- Is there a logical leap?
- Are unstated assumptions necessary?
- Does evidence actually support conclusion?

**Common disconnects:**
- Correlation data → Causal claim
- Sample finding → Population claim
- Preliminary result → Definitive conclusion
- Mechanism proposed → Mechanism demonstrated

**Example of disconnect:**
> **Data**: "Blood pressure decreased 5mmHg in treatment group (p=.04)"
>
> **Claim**: "This treatment prevents cardiovascular disease"
>
> **Problem**: Surrogate outcome (BP) ≠ clinical outcome (CVD). Small effect. Single study.

### 4. Evaluate Proportionality

**Is confidence proportional to evidence?**

**Well-calibrated claims:**
- Acknowledge uncertainty
- Use appropriate hedging ("may", "suggests")
- Note limitations
- Avoid overstatement

**Poorly calibrated claims:**
- Absolute language ("proves", "demonstrates conclusively")
- Ignoring uncertainty
- Dismissing limitations
- Overgeneralizing

**Language red flags:**
- "Proves" (rarely appropriate)
- "Clearly shows" (often overstated)
- "Definitively demonstrates" (very high bar)
- "No doubt that" (ignoring uncertainty)

### 5. Check for Overgeneralization

**Population generalization:**
- Does sample represent claimed population?
- Are restrictions acknowledged?
- Example: College students → "people"

**Condition generalization:**
- Do lab conditions match real world?
- Are contextual limits noted?
- Example: High-dose lab study → dietary intake

**Outcome generalization:**
- Does measured outcome match claimed outcome?
- Surrogate vs. clinical outcomes?
- Example: Biomarker → disease outcome

**Temporal generalization:**
- Short-term findings → long-term claims?
- Follow-up duration sufficient?
- Example: 8-week study → "lasting benefits"

### 6. Red Flags

**Strong red flags (major problems):**
- Causal language from correlational studies
- "Proves" or absolute certainty
- Cherry-picked citations
- Ignoring contradictory evidence
- Dismissing all limitations
- Extrapolating far beyond data

**Moderate red flags (worth noting):**
- Mechanistic speculation presented as fact
- Single study generalized broadly
- Surrogate outcomes as clinical outcomes
- Preliminary findings stated too confidently

**Minor flags (common limitations):**
- Slight overstatement in discussion
- Abstract stronger than results support
- Media reports exaggerating findings

## Claim Evaluation Rubric

### Well-Supported Claim
✓ Evidence directly addresses claim
✓ Multiple high-quality studies
✓ Alternative explanations ruled out
✓ Appropriate hedging language
✓ Limitations acknowledged
✓ Generalization justified

### Moderately Supported Claim
✓ Some direct evidence
≈ Limited replication
≈ Some alternative explanations remain
≈ Mostly appropriate language
≈ Some limitations acknowledged
≈ Generalization somewhat justified

### Poorly Supported Claim
✗ Indirect or weak evidence
✗ Single preliminary study
✗ Alternative explanations not addressed
✗ Overconfident language
✗ Limitations dismissed
✗ Overgeneralized

## Providing Feedback

**Structure:**

1. **Quote the Claim**: Cite exact wording
2. **Assess Evidence**: Summarize supporting evidence
3. **Evaluate Match**: Does evidence support claim strength?
4. **Identify Issues**: Logical leaps, overgeneralizations, etc.
5. **Suggest Revision**: How to make claim proportional

**Example:**

> **Claim**: "This study proves that meditation cures depression" (Abstract, line 12)
>
> **Evidence**: Single RCT (n=60) showing 8-point reduction in BDI-II scores (moderate effect size) at 8 weeks. No follow-up. No comparison to standard treatment. Self-reported outcomes.
>
> **Issues**:
> 1. "Proves" is too strong—single study cannot prove
> 2. "Cures" is overstated—reduction in symptoms ≠ cure
> 3. Generalization beyond sample (recruited volunteers, may not represent all depression)
> 4. Short-term outcome, long-term unknown
>
> **Suggested revision**: "This preliminary RCT (n=60) found that an 8-week meditation intervention reduced depressive symptoms (measured by BDI-II) compared to waitlist control. Effect size was moderate (d=0.62). Longer-term outcomes and comparison to evidence-based treatments remain to be established."

## Common Claim-Evidence Mismatches

| Claim | Evidence | Problem |
|-------|----------|---------|
| "X causes Y" | Correlation study | Cannot infer causation |
| "Treatment is effective" | Surrogate outcome only | Need clinical outcomes |
| "Effect is proven" | Single study | Need replication |
| "Works for everyone" | Specific sample | Overgeneralization |
| "Long-term benefits" | Short-term study | Temporal overgeneralization |
| "Safe" | No adverse events detected | Absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence |

## Key Principles

1. **Claims should match evidence strength**
2. **Uncertainty should be acknowledged**
3. **Limitations should be noted, not dismissed**
4. **Generalization should be justified**
5. **Alternative explanations should be addressed**
6. **Preliminary findings should be labeled as such**

## Remember

**Distinguish between:**
- What was found (data)
- What it might mean (interpretation)
- What we can conclude (claims)

**Good science:**
- Makes claims proportional to evidence
- Acknowledges uncertainty
- Notes limitations honestly
- Avoids overgeneralization
- Distinguishes findings from speculation
